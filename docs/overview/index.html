Overview


2020 Halifax County Summer Project

The United States incarcerates more people per capita than anywhere else in the world1, and a disproportionate number of those incarcerated are Black and Hispanic2. Estimates from the ACLU suggest that one of every three Black males and one out of every six Latino males will be incarcerated, compared to only one out of seventeen for white males.

In addition to the drastic human costs incurred by these mass incarceration policies, the current incarceration rate is extremely costly for taxpayers. The U.S. spends roughtly $60 to $80 billion per year on state and federal prisons3 4. But despite these expenditures, roughly two-thirds of individuals released from prison were re-arrested within three years5.

Halifax County has partnered with DSPG 2020 to investigate the factors associated with its incarceration and recidivism rate in the hopes of investing in proactive services through the Virginia Cooperative Extension Service, like a Family and Consumer Science Agent. Among other things, this position would help connect formerly incarcerated individuals to the community resources needed to help them effectively re-enter society.

Project Goals

As this project has only just gotten underway, our primary goals for the summer have been to synthesize a variety of prior sociological research to identify the most impactful social determinants of incarceration and recidivism.

We then sought to identify accessible data sources that may allow us to characterize the state of these determinants in Halifax County itself.

We hope that identifying emerging patterns in these determinants will allow Halifax to more specifically target those factors that are most relevant to the state of incarceration and recidivism in the county.

Our Approach

We are using the social-ecological model6 to guide our understanding of the factors related to incarceration in Halifax County. This model frames individual behavior as the result of influences that act at multiple sociological levels, including neighborhood, community, and policy levels. For example, through the lens of this model, a re-entering indivdual's ability to maintain employment might consider the accessibility of employment opportunities, opportunities for continued vocational training, and policies surrounding employment eligibility for those with prior criminal convictions.

Under this framework, our approach centered around an extensive literature review of prior research in the area, a comprehensive exploration and accumulation of available data sources on the most important factors impacting incarceration, and preliminary data visualization to communicate the state of these factors in Halifax County, VA.

While it is difficult to make definitive conclusions on a topic as nuanced and complex as incarceration, we hope that this work will provide a foundation for Halifax County to better understand the factors it may seek to target as it attempts to improve the state of incarceration within its borders, connect the county with data sources that may be useful in evaluating the problem, and identify the data that may be required were a more comprehensive view of the issue to emerge.
